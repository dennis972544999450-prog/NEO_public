# R09 — "Primes as a Spectrum": Random-Matrix Statistics of Riemann Zeros and the Random Model of the Primes

## TL;DR
- **The intuition that fails, and its measurable price:** Local statistics of Riemann zeros match Random Matrix Theory (RMT) *universally*, but *global/finite-height* statistics carry arithmetic corrections; and the "primes ≈ independent coin flips" (Cramér) intuition is *provably* wrong in short intervals (Maier, a theorem) and in consecutive-residue-class correlations (Lemke Oliver–Soundararajan). The price of naive extrapolation from the finite/local/commutative is real and, in several cases, quantified.
- **State of the field (2023–2026):** The load-bearing theorems (Montgomery pair correlation under RH; Rudnick–Sarnak n-level; Maier; Rubinstein–Sarnak) are decades old and unbeaten. Recent work is *refinement* — moment lower-order terms, unconditional Chebyshev biases in number fields, a definitive comparative-PNT bibliography and open-problem list, sharper variance-in-short-intervals bounds — not a paradigm shift. No new record-height zero computation has surpassed Odlyzko (~10²³) / Gourdon (~10²⁴) since ~2004.
- **What a single-GPU / Colab run can actually touch:** reproducing/extending the Lemke Oliver–Soundararajan consecutive-prime bias (best novelty-per-compute), computing Rubinstein–Sarnak densities from low zeros, re-analyzing pair-correlation + arithmetic corrections on *public* zero data, and testing CFKRS moment polynomials at moderate height. Proving anything, or beating existing zero-computation records, is out of reach.

## Key Findings

**1. Zero statistics.** Montgomery's pair correlation conjecture (1973) is a *theorem* only for test functions with Fourier support in (−1,1), under RH; the full conjecture is open. Rudnick–Sarnak (1996) extended this to n-level correlations of principal L-functions, again with restricted support, and this remains the state of the art. The Keating–Snaith (2000) CUE-characteristic-polynomial model produced the first conjectural moment constant g_k for all k; CFKRS (2005) gave the full moment polynomial of degree k²; CFKRS (2008) computed lower-order terms. Bogomolny–Keating (1995/96) derived the *arithmetic* lower-order corrections to correlations from the Hardy–Littlewood conjectures, and these match Odlyzko's data well qualitatively. Everything here is conjectural except the leading terms for k=1,2.

**2. Cramér failures.** Maier's theorem (1985) is the load-bearing negative result: primes in intervals of length (log x)^λ do NOT match Cramér predictions. Granville's refinement (divisibility by small primes) and Montgomery–Soundararajan (2004, Gaussian variance smaller than Cramér's) fix specific discrepancies. Lemke Oliver–Soundararajan (2016) found the consecutive-prime residue-class bias, explained conjecturally via Hardy–Littlewood.

**3. Chebyshev's bias.** Rubinstein–Sarnak (1994): under GRH + LI, δ(4;3,1) = 0.9959…. Fiorilli–Martin (2013) gave the asymptotic formula for densities. Fiorilli–Jouve (2022) proved *unconditional* extreme biases in number fields. The unconditional existence of the classic mod-4 race density without GRH/LI remains open.

## Details

### Part 1 — Beyond GUE pair correlation

**Montgomery's pair correlation conjecture (conjecture; partial theorem).** Montgomery (1973, "The pair correlation of zeros of the zeta function," Proc. Symp. Pure Math. 24, 181–193) studied the form factor F(α,T) and proved, *assuming RH*, that the pair correlation matches the GUE sine-kernel 1 − (sin πu/πu)² for test functions whose Fourier transform is supported in (−1,1). Extending the support beyond 1 is equivalent to deep information about primes in short intervals (Goldston–Montgomery equivalence — a theorem). The Dyson–Montgomery "teatime" identification of the GUE kernel is well documented. CITING.

**GUE hypothesis (conjecture).** Normalized spacings of zeros follow the GUE eigenvalue distribution asymptotically. Not proven; strongly supported numerically (Odlyzko).

**Rudnick–Sarnak (theorem, restricted).** Rudnick–Sarnak, "Zeros of principal L-functions and random matrix theory," Duke Math. J. 81 (1996), 269–322, proved the n-level correlations of zeros of principal (automorphic) L-functions agree with GUE for test functions with restricted Fourier support (unconditional for correlation degree m ≤ 3, otherwise under their "Hypothesis H" on prime-power coefficients). This is the fundamental generalization of Montgomery to higher correlations and to the whole automorphic family. HIGH confidence (theorem, explicitly conditional on support/H).

**Keating–Snaith (conjecture, RMT-derived).** Keating–Snaith, "Random matrix theory and ζ(1/2+it)," Comm. Math. Phys. 214 (2000), 57–89. They model ζ near height T by the characteristic polynomial Z(θ,A)=det(I−Ae^{−iθ}) of an N×N CUE matrix with N ≈ log(T/2π), and conjecture the 2k-th moment
∫₀ᵀ |ζ(½+it)|^{2k} dt ∼ a(k)·[G²(k+1)/G(2k+1)]·(log T/2π)^{k²},
where a(k) is the arithmetic (Euler-product) factor and the Barnes-G ratio is the RMT factor. This unified/explained the earlier c₃ (Conrey–Ghosh) and c₄ (Conrey–Gonek) conjectures, whose mysterious integer constants were g₃ = 42 and g₄ = 24024. Proven only for k=1 (Hardy–Littlewood 1918) and k=2 (Ingham 1926). CONJECTURE.

**CFKRS (conjecture).** Conrey–Farmer–Keating–Rubinstein–Snaith, "Integral moments of L-functions," Proc. LMS 91 (2005), 33–104. The "recipe" gives the *full* moment asymptotic as a polynomial P_k of degree k² in log T (not just the leading term), matching CUE characteristic-polynomial moments. CFKRS, "Lower order terms in the full moment conjecture for the Riemann zeta function," J. Number Theory 128 (2008), 1516–1554 (arXiv:math/0612843), gives an algorithm for the lower-order coefficients plus numerical evidence. **Practical trap:** the leading coefficient a_k·g_k/Γ(k²+1) shrinks rapidly with k, so at accessible heights T the lower-order terms can *dominate* the "main" term — a genuine hazard for anyone testing moments numerically at moderate T (noted explicitly in the moments literature, e.g. Soundararajan's "distribution of values" survey, arXiv:2112.03389).

**Bogomolny–Keating arithmetic corrections (conjecture/heuristic).** Bogomolny–Keating, "Random matrix theory and the Riemann zeros I & II," Nonlinearity 8 (1995), 1115–1131 and 9 (1996), 911–935. Using the Hardy–Littlewood prime-pair conjecture (and later a Gutzwiller-trace-formula method), they derived the finite-height, arithmetic lower-order terms in the n-point correlations. Quoting Conrey–Snaith (arXiv:0803.2795): "A close examination of Odlyzko's data revealed that lower order terms, likely to be of an arithmetic nature, were present… They gave full details for the lower order terms for the 2-point correlation, as well as numerics showing the goodness of fit." Conrey–Snaith (2008, "Correlations of eigenvalues and Riemann zeros," arXiv:0803.2795) rederived all these lower-order terms uniformly from the Conrey–Farmer–Zirnbauer ratios conjecture. Bogomolny states the Hardy–Littlewood-derived 2-point formula "agrees very well with numerical calculations of Odlyzko." There is a "resurgent" relationship (Berry; Bogomolny–Keating): the finite-T deviations of the *high* zeros' statistics are governed by the *low* zeros. **INFERENCE (mine):** the arithmetic-correction program is the concrete instance of the OMPU thesis for this site — intuition trained on the universal (RMT) limit pays a measurable price at finite height, and that price is exactly the arithmetic (prime) information. CONJECTURE/HEURISTIC.

**Equivalence to twin primes.** Conrey–Keating, "Pair correlation and twin primes revisited," Proc. R. Soc. A 2016 (arXiv:1604.06124), showed the Hardy–Littlewood twin-prime constant can be recovered from the pair-correlation-with-lower-order-terms; the two conjectures are heuristically equivalent — a two-way bridge between zero statistics and prime correlations. (Goldston–Montgomery earlier proved the pair-correlation conjecture equivalent to a short-interval variance asymptotic; Montgomery–Soundararajan proved that variance follows from Hardy–Littlewood.)

**Large-height computations (empirical status).**
- Odlyzko, "On the distribution of spacings between zeros of the zeta function," Math. Comp. 48 (1987), 273–308: first 10⁵ zeros and zeros #10¹²+1…10¹²+10⁵ (accuracy ±10⁻⁸, on Cray-1/X-MP). HIGH confidence.
- Odlyzko, "The 10²²-nd zero of the Riemann zeta function," AMS Contemp. Math. 290 (2001), 139–144: ~10 billion zeros near the 10²²-nd zero. HIGH confidence.
- Odlyzko (unpublished, 2002): ~20 billion zeros near the 10²³-rd zero — the most extensive dataset for GUE testing; documented only *secondhand* via Gourdon (2004) and Forrester–Trinh (2015). MODERATE confidence (unpublished).
- Gourdon (2004, unrefereed manuscript, with Demichel): verified RH for the **first 10¹³ (ten trillion) zeros**; computed 2×10⁹ zeros near the 10²⁴-th zero, via an optimized Odlyzko–Schönhage algorithm. MODERATE confidence (unrefereed).
- Bober–Hiary, "New computations of the Riemann zeta function on the critical line," arXiv:1607.00709 (2016): focused on large *values* of |ζ| and high zeros using Hiary's fast quadratic-exponential-sum algorithm (split over ~130,000 blocks). They checked RH "for more than 50000 zeros in over 200 separate small intervals with values of t ranging from 10²⁴ to 8×10³⁴" — confirming no new *contiguous* record set post-2004. Not a record contiguous zero dataset.
- Platt–Trudgian, "The Riemann hypothesis is true up to 3·10¹²," Bull. LMS 53 (2021), 792–797 (arXiv:2004.09765): the *rigorous* interval-arithmetic record — exactly 12,363,153,437,138 zeros confirmed on the line up to height 3,000,175,332,800, all simple. HIGH confidence.
- **2023–2026:** No new record-height zero dataset for GUE/pair-correlation testing. Recent pair-correlation work (e.g., Baluyot–Goldston–Suriajaya–Turnage-Butterbaugh 2025) is analytic, not numerical. HIGH confidence on the negative claim; MODERATE on exhaustiveness.
- Forrester–Trinh (Proc. R. Soc. A 471 (2015), 20150436, arXiv:1506.06531) used Odlyzko's ~10²³ dataset (>10⁹ consecutive zeros) to compare the empirical two-point correlation to a finite-size correction identical in form to the 1/N² CUE correction. HIGH confidence.

**How well do the arithmetic corrections match numerics?** MODERATE-to-HIGH confidence qualitatively: multiple authoritative sources call the fit "goodness of fit shown numerically" (Conrey–Snaith), "agrees very well" (Bogomolny), "remarkably accurate model of the data" (Bourgade–Keating survey). **LOW confidence on any single published quantitative residual** — I did not find a stated χ² or percent-error figure in the Bogomolny–Keating comparison itself; the agreement is characterized qualitatively. The deviation structure in the spacing distribution at height ~10²³ was explained via finite-size/arithmetic corrections by Bogomolny–Bohigas–Leboeuf–Monastra, J. Phys. A 39 (2006), 10743.

### Part 2 — Failures of the Cramér model

**Cramér model (heuristic).** Cramér (1936, "On the order of magnitude of the difference between consecutive prime numbers," Acta Arith. 2, 23–46) modeled n as "prime" with independent probability 1/log n. Predicts correctly at PNT scale and max gap ∼ (log x)²; predicts *incorrectly* fine short-interval behavior.

**Maier's theorem (theorem, 1985).** Maier, "Primes in short intervals," Michigan Math. J. 32 (1985), 221–225. For any λ > 1, the ratio [π(x + (log x)^λ) − π(x)] / [(log x)^λ / log x] does NOT tend to 1; limsup > 1 and liminf < 1. Cramér's model predicts limit 1 for λ ≥ 2. Proof via the "Maier matrix method" (Buchstab/quasi-prime weighting + Gallagher's theorem on primes in APs). This is the canonical *theorem* that the independent-coin-flip intuition fails at the (log x)^λ scale. Pintz (2007, "Cramér vs. Cramér," Funct. Approx. Comment. Math. 37, 361–376) gave another proof and showed that most Cramér-type models with independent variables incorrectly predict the short-interval mean square. HIGH confidence (theorem).

**Granville refinement (heuristic).** Granville (1995, "Harald Cramér and the distribution of prime numbers," Scand. Actuarial J. 1) modified the model so that only integers coprime to small primes carry the elevated probability. This shifts constants and repairs part of the Maier discrepancy. Quoting Ford–Green–Konyagin–Maynard–Tao (JAMS 2018): "Granville offered a refinement of Cramér's model and has conjectured that the limsup above is in fact at least 2e^{−γ} = 1.1229…" (versus Cramér's original c = 1). CITING.

**Montgomery–Soundararajan (conditional theorem + conjecture, 2004).** "Primes in short intervals," Comm. Math. Phys. 252 (2004), 589–617 (arXiv:math/0409258). ψ(x+H) − ψ(x) is approximately Gaussian with mean ∼H and variance ∼ H·log(N/H) for N^δ ≤ H ≤ N^{1−δ} — contrary to the Cramér variance ∼ H. They prove the 2k-th moment asymptotics conditional on a strong uniform Hardy–Littlewood k-tuple conjecture. The variance being *smaller* than Cramér predicts is among the sharpest quantitative statements of the failure. Follow-up (2021–2024): de la Bretèche–Fiorilli (Math. Ann. 381 (2021), 575–591) and de la Bretèche–Kuperberg (arXiv:2305.02662) proved lower bounds on weighted even moments matching the conjecture (unconditional for an unbounded set of X, all X under RH); Parry (arXiv:2409.00431) pushed the third-moment exponent toward the conjectured h^{1+o(1)}.

**Prime gaps.**
- *Large gaps (theorem, 2014–2018):* Ford–Green–Konyagin–Tao and (independently) Maynard broke the Erdős–Rankin barrier; the combined Ford–Green–Konyagin–Maynard–Tao, "Long gaps between primes," JAMS 31 (2018), 65–105 (arXiv:1412.5029), proved G(X) ≫ log X · log log X · log log log log X / log log log X. Key new ingredient: a Rödl-nibble generalization of the Pippenger–Spencer hypergraph covering theorem. Won the Erdős $10,000 prize. HIGH confidence (theorem).
- *Small/bounded gaps (theorem, 2013–2014):* Zhang, "Bounded gaps between primes," Annals of Math. 179 (2014), 1121–1174, proved liminf(p_{n+1} − p_n) < 7×10⁷ (70,000,000). Maynard, "Small gaps between primes," Ann. Math. 181 (2015), 383–413 (arXiv:1311.4600), reduced the bound to 600 and proved H_m finite for all m; Polymath8b reduced H₁ ≤ 246 (≤ 12 under Elliott–Halberstam, ≤ 6 under generalized EH). Twin prime conjecture (H₁ = 2) remains OPEN. Tao's 2023 survey (arXiv:2306.01107) is the current overview. HIGH confidence (theorems).

**Lemke Oliver–Soundararajan bias (2016).** "Unexpected biases in the distribution of consecutive primes," PNAS 113 (2016), E4446–E4454 (arXiv:1603.03720). Consecutive primes avoid repeating the same residue class far more than a naive model predicts (e.g., mod 10, in the first 10⁸ pairs, ~4.6 million end {1,1} vs. >7.4 million ending {1,3}). Explained conjecturally via Hardy–Littlewood with a large secondary term; fit to data is very good. Extended to sums of two squares (David–Devin–Nam–Schlitt, Math. Ann. 2021, arXiv:2105.05048). This is the freshest genuinely "surprising" Cramér-failure and the best target for a small-compute reproduction. **Controversy flag:** Holt (arXiv:2405.03540, 2024) argues the biases are "totally expected" from the evolution of gap populations under Eratosthenes sieve — i.e., a finite-range artifact, not an asymptotic mystery. This is a live interpretive dispute (see Part 3 critiques).

### Part 3 — Chebyshev's bias

**Rubinstein–Sarnak (1994, conditional framework).** "Chebyshev's bias," Experiment. Math. 3 (1994), 173–197. Under GRH + LI (linear independence over ℚ of the imaginary parts of zeros of the relevant Dirichlet L-functions — a.k.a. the "grand simplicity hypothesis," which also implies all zeros are simple), the logarithmic density δ(q;a,b) exists. They computed δ(4;3,1) = 0.9959… and δ(3;2,1) = 0.9990…, with the sign rule "δ(q;a,b) > 1/2 if and only if a is a nonsquare (mod q) and b is a square (mod q)." For π(x) vs Li(x), δ ≈ 0.99999973. As q→∞ (many contestants) biases dissolve. This is the foundational framework of comparative prime number theory. CONDITIONAL THEOREM.

**Fiorilli–Martin (2013).** "Inequities in the Shanks–Rényi prime number race: an asymptotic formula for the densities," J. Reine Angew. Math. 676 (2013), 121–212 (arXiv:0912.4908). Under GRH + LI: an asymptotic series for δ(q;a,b) with error smaller than any negative power of q, expressed via a variance V(q;a,b) reducible from an infinite sum over zeros to a finite expression; explains which races are more biased from arithmetic (e.g., if a is a prime power and a′ is not, δ(q;a,1) < δ(q;a′,1) for all but finitely many q). Extensive rigorous numerical density tables (all 117 densities exceeding a threshold, etc.). CONDITIONAL.

**Fiorilli "Highly biased races" (2014).** Algebra & Number Theory 8 (2014), 1733–1767 (arXiv:1210.6946). Densities can be arbitrarily close to 1; the mechanism is that L-functions with zeros close to the real axis produce *lower* bias, while a high first zero produces higher bias — so the π(x)-vs-Li(x) density (0.99999973…) is not the ceiling. CONDITIONAL.

**Unconditional results.** Fiorilli–Jouve, "Unconditional Chebyshev biases in number fields," J. Éc. polytech. Math. 9 (2022), 671–679 (arXiv:2012.12245): *unconditionally* exhibit *extreme* biases in the Chebotarev setting, beyond what the strongest effective forms of Chebotarev can predict. Devin (2020, Math. Proc. Camb. Phil. Soc. 169, 103–140, arXiv:1706.06394) established the limiting distribution for analytic L-functions under hypotheses much weaker than full LI (yielding, e.g., an unconditional limiting distribution for the π(x)–Li(x) race). **HONEST NEGATIVE:** for the classic π(x;4,3) vs π(x;4,1) race, an unconditional proof that the *density* exists (without GRH/LI) is still not available; infinitude of sign changes (Littlewood-type) is classical, but the density statement is conditional. OPEN.

**Recent (2023–2026).**
- Martin, Yang, et al., "An annotated bibliography for comparative prime number theory," arXiv:2309.08729 (v3, Dec 2024; published Expo. Math. 43 (2025), 125644): the definitive literature map, cataloguing every publication through mid-2024 with unified notation. **Start here.**
- Hamieh–Kadiri–Martin–Ng, "Comparative Prime Number Theory Problem List," arXiv:2407.03530 (from the June 2024 UBC/PIMS Comparative Prime Number Theory Symposium): the current open-problem set.
- Function-field / Deep Riemann Hypothesis (DRH) strand: Aoki–Koyama (J. Number Theory 245 (2023), 233–262); Kaneko–Koyama (Proc. AMS 151 (2023), 5059–5069, arXiv:2206.05445); Okumura, "Chebyshev's bias for Fermat curves of prime degree," Ramanujan J. 65 (2024), 725–742 (arXiv:2307.05958); Bailleul, "Exceptional biases in counting primes over function fields," J. LMS (2024, arXiv:2302.13665); "Ties in Function Field Prime Races," arXiv:2603.21005 (2026); "Prime Ideal Races With Several Competitors," arXiv:2508.04087 (2025).
- Suzuki, "On variants of Chebyshev's conjecture," Ramanujan J. 68 (2025); Sheth, "Euler Products at the Centre and Applications to Chebyshev's Bias," Math. Proc. Camb. Phil. Soc. 179 (2025).

## Recommendations — experiments ranked by tractability (single-GPU / Colab)

Each lists its NULL-CASE *first* (the concrete observation that would kill or demote it), then the payoff.

**Experiment A — Lemke Oliver–Soundararajan bias reproduction & extension (highest novelty-per-compute).**
- **NULL-CASE:** If, on the first 10⁸–10⁹ primes, the consecutive-class transition counts (mod q for q ∈ {4,10,12,…}) match the LOS Hardy–Littlewood secondary-term prediction to within Monte-Carlo/normalization error *and* show no residual structure beyond it, there is nothing new — demote to "confirmation of LOS." Moreover, if the observed bias is fully accounted for by Holt's gap-evolution / "next prime comes sooner" mechanism (arXiv:2405.03540), the effect is a finite-range artifact, not deep.
- **Payoff if not-null:** a modulus or range where the LOS model *fails*, or residual structure the Hardy–Littlewood secondary term does not capture, would be genuinely publishable. Sieve to 10⁹ on GPU, count consecutive-class pairs, compare to the LOS formula and to Holt's prediction to disentangle them. HIGH feasibility.

**Experiment B — Rubinstein–Sarnak densities from low zeros.**
- **NULL-CASE:** If your Monte-Carlo of the limiting distribution (built from the first few thousand zeros of L(s,χ₋₄), etc., via LMFDB) reproduces δ(4;3,1)=0.9959… and Fiorilli–Martin's tables to 3–4 digits, you have merely reconfirmed known values. If you cannot reach that precision, the run is under-powered and says nothing.
- **Payoff:** densities for moduli/L-functions absent from existing tables, or a numerical LI stress-test (searching for near-linear-dependences among low ordinates) flagging a *candidate* LI failure. MODERATE feasibility.

**Experiment C — Pair-correlation / nearest-neighbor spacing on public zero data + arithmetic correction.**
- **NULL-CASE:** If Odlyzko's *published* spacing data (first 10⁵ zeros; blocks at 10¹², 10²¹, 10²²) reproduce the GUE sine-kernel and the Bogomolny–Keating lower-order term with no residual, you have reconfirmed 1987–2001 results — demote.
- **Payoff:** a clean finite-height fit isolating the arithmetic term across multiple heights, or a discrepancy in a higher (3-, 4-level) correlation vs the Conrey–Snaith ratios prediction. MODERATE on public data. **Note:** computing *new* high zeros yourself is NOT single-GPU tractable — Odlyzko and Gourdon used the Odlyzko–Schönhage algorithm on large clusters.

**Experiment D — CFKRS moment-polynomial test at moderate height.**
- **NULL-CASE:** If ∫₀ᵀ|ζ(½+it)|^{2k} dt / P_k(log T) → 1 for k=1,2,3 at accessible T (≲10⁸ via Riemann–Siegel/Hiary), matching CFKRS including lower-order terms, you've reconfirmed. If the leading-term-only Keating–Snaith prediction fits *better* than the full CFKRS polynomial, that merely signals you're below the asymptotic regime (expected — lower-order terms dominate at small T).
- **Payoff:** a clean demonstration that lower-order terms are *necessary* at accessible heights (pedagogically valuable; mirrors CFKRS 2008 numerics). MODERATE feasibility for k ≤ 3.

**Experiment E — Maier-phenomenon illustration (almost certainly null at Colab scale).**
- **NULL-CASE:** A brute-force count of primes in intervals of length (log x)^λ will NOT visibly violate Cramér at accessible x — Maier's oscillation is asymptotic and tiny at small x. Concretely, real data still sits *below* Cramér's ceiling: the greatest known value of g_n/log²p_n is only 0.9206386… (for the prime 1693182318746371, per LeClair, arXiv:1506.03359) — i.e. you cannot even reach c=1, let alone see the Maier oscillation. A run showing "ratio ≈ 1" does not refute Maier; it confirms you're below the regime.
- **Payoff:** pedagogical only. LOW feasibility for a genuine test.

**Staged plan.** Do A first (highest novelty-per-compute), then C on public data, then B. **Thresholds that change the plan:** if A shows residual structure beyond LOS/Holt at 10⁸, scale to 10¹⁰ and formalize; if C shows any deviation from Conrey–Snaith at the 3-level, prioritize that immediately.

## Caveats

- **Confidence grades.** Theorems (Montgomery-restricted, Rudnick–Sarnak-restricted, Maier, Montgomery–Soundararajan-conditional, FGKMT large gaps, Zhang/Maynard small gaps, Fiorilli–Jouve unconditional number-field biases) are HIGH-confidence and explicitly marked. The GUE hypothesis, Keating–Snaith/CFKRS moments, Bogomolny–Keating corrections, and Rubinstein–Sarnak/Fiorilli–Martin densities are CONJECTURAL (conditional on RH/GRH/LI and/or Hardy–Littlewood) — marked as such. The "arithmetic corrections fit Odlyzko well" claim is MODERATE-HIGH qualitatively, LOW on any single numeric residual.
- **Numerical-regime trap (the single most important practical warning).** For moments (CFKRS) and for Maier, accessible heights/lengths are BELOW the asymptotic regime; lower-order terms dominate and naive fits mislead. Build every experiment expecting this.
- **Sourcing gaps I am flagging.** Odlyzko's 10²³ / 20-billion-zero dataset is UNPUBLISHED (2002), documented only secondhand (Gourdon 2004; Forrester–Trinh 2015). Gourdon 2004 is an unrefereed manuscript. Granville's 2e^{−γ} constant is cited via FGKMT and secondary summaries, not verified line-by-line against the 1995 Scandinavian Actuarial Journal original.

### Adjacent fringe claims to steer around (with the reason each is fringe)
- **Hilbert–Pólya overreach.** The conjecture (zeros = eigenvalues of a self-adjoint operator) is a legitimate heuristic and motivates Berry–Keating H = xp; but claims that a specific physical Hamiltonian has been found that *proves* RH are fringe. The Berry–Keating and Bender–Brody–Müller (PRL 118, 130201, 2017) proposals are suggestive, NOT proofs — the operator is not known to be self-adjoint in the required sense. Steer around any "RH proved via quantum mechanics" claim.
- **"Quantum chaos proves RH."** The GUE connection is evidence and analogy, not proof. Rudnick–Sarnak and Katz–Sarnak are rigorous about *what* is proven (restricted-support correlations, function-field analogues); anyone asserting the physics closes RH is overclaiming.
- **de Branges' claimed proofs.** Louis de Branges has repeatedly posted claimed RH proofs; consensus (Conrey–Li exhibited an explicit counterexample to the positivity his approach requires) is that they are not accepted. Do not build on them.
- **Zeta-zero numerology.** Claims of hidden musical/fractal/patterned structure in individual zero ordinates beyond the established RMT statistics are fringe: the zeros' local statistics are GUE and their global density is Riemann–von Mangoldt — there is no established "extra" structure.
- **Deep Riemann Hypothesis (DRH) strand — caution, not dismissal.** The Kurokawa/Koyama/Aoki/Okumura DRH-based Chebyshev-bias papers (2022–2025) appear in reputable venues but rest on a strong, non-standard convergence conjecture for partial Euler products on the critical line. Treat their conclusions as conditional on DRH, which is not widely regarded as established. Not fringe, but heavily conditional.
- **"Cramér's conjecture is a theorem" / elementary disproofs of Maier.** Occasional preprints (e.g., a 2011 note claiming a "fatal fault" in Maier's proof) claim to overturn Maier's theorem; these are not accepted — Maier's theorem is correct and independently reproved (Pintz 2007). Steer around.

### Live controversies / who disputes what
- **Interpretation of the LOS bias:** Lemke Oliver–Soundararajan (Hardy–Littlewood secondary term, a "real" phenomenon) vs. Holt (arXiv:2405.03540: "totally expected" from finite-range sieve/gap-evolution). Both are correct at the level of formulas; the dispute is about whether the effect is a deep asymptotic bias or a finite-range artifact. This is the most directly *testable* controversy for a small-compute program (Experiment A).
- **What RMT actually establishes about RH:** the number-theory community (Sarnak, Conrey) treats the RMT connection as a source of conjectures, not a route to a proof; physics-adjacent claims periodically overstate it.
